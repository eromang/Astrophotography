## Imaging Performance

With the [[Nikon-D5300]] (3.89 µm pixels, APS-C sensor):

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Image scale | 0.64"/pixel |
| Field of view | ~0.28° x 0.19° |
| Sampling | Heavily oversampled for typical 2-4" seeing |

> **Note:** At 0.64"/pixel, the setup is oversampled by ~4x for typical seeing conditions. 2x2 software binning (post-capture) would bring it to 1.28"/pixel, which is more practical. See [[Pixel-Binning]].

## Characteristics

### Strengths

- **Long focal length** — 1250mm resolves lunar craters, planetary detail, and tight double stars
- **Compact design** — Maksutov-Cassegrain is short and portable
- **GoTo mount** — automated object location, useful for visual observation
- **Low maintenance** — sealed tube, no collimation needed

### Limitations

- **Alt-azimuth mount** — field rotation prevents long-exposure deep sky imaging; limits exposures to a few seconds without a wedge
- **Slow f/14** — requires very long exposures for faint objects (impractical for deep sky)
- **No equatorial tracking** — cannot guide; no autoguider port
- **Small aperture** — 90mm gathers limited light compared to larger scopes

## Best Use Cases

| Use case | Suitability |
|----------|-------------|
| Lunar imaging | Excellent — high resolution, short exposures |
| Planetary (Jupiter, Saturn, Mars) | Good — lucky imaging with video capture |
| Visual observation | Good — GoTo for finding objects, decent aperture for visual |
| Deep sky imaging | Poor — f/14, alt-az mount, no guiding |
| Double stars | Good — high magnification, compact |

### Planetary Imaging Workflow

1. Use [[ASIStudio]] ASIVideoStack or similar for lucky imaging
2. Capture short video (30-120s) at high frame rate
3. Stack best 10-20% of frames
4. Sharpen with wavelets
